Novice

Things to Know

Don’t Bring Down Your Site

Anyone who hasn’t done a migration before should consider a few things. First, and most importantly, never alter files or databases on the source (the original server). The source version of your website should, ideally, stay online during your migration. If your web hosting subscription is running out and aren’t certain you have enough time to complete your migration end-to-end, consider extending your hosting. A proper migration copies files and databases, consider this procedure as more of a clone and less of a move. That being said you should always keep a backup of your site through one means or another, especially during migration but also a best practice. A lot of the plugins I mention below handle making backups and there are a lot of great backup plugins that are free!

You haven’t botched a migration unless you’ve brought down or broken your live website. If your business or livelihood depends on your site having minimal downtime, please take extra care during this transition. Bringing down your website could also affect your SEO in a negative way.

DNS and Domains

Next, a word on DNS and domains. Before and during a migration it’s not a good idea to update DNS or start a domain transfer to your new host. You may accidentally bring down your website and/or email by doing so unless your migration is already complete. If you aren’t sure what DNS is or how yours works talk to your web hosting support team. In short, DNS is the mechanism that directs internet traffic to your web hosting server when they type your domain into their browser. Once your migration is done then DNS will need to be updated, reach out to your support team if you need direction.

Anatomy of a WordPress Site

All WordPress sites are made of two major parts: the website files and the database. In the document root of your WordPress site is a configuration file named wp-config.php which contains the database connection strings that will need to be updated on the new server. If you’re using a plugin you won’t likely need to touch this file. Also inside your document root are three main directories or folders that WordPress needs. The first, and most important to you, is the wp-content folder, which has your uploads (media), plugins, and themes. The second and third are part of the WordPress core: wp-includes and wp-admin.

The WordPress database is likely (((BOLD the most important part))) of your WordPress site, other than your uploads. The database holds all the things that make your site unique, including all your page content. The WordPress database is composed of several tables, though you may not need to worry about what’s inside as much as everything being there during the migration.

Consider your website a paperweight if you only have the files without the database or visa-versa. Some of the WordPress files can be replaced, advanced migration methods allow you to only migrate what’s necessary and replace the rest. For the novice migrator, consider all your files important and your database as critical.

Previewing the Migrated Version

Because we’ve taken extra care to ensure our website stays online during this transition you may find it difficult to preview the site on the new server. With WordPress, there are two reliable ways to do this: updating the hosts file on your computer or setting your WP site on a temporary URL. Both have their pros and cons.

On Windows, Mac, and Linux, within your system files, there is a particular file we’re interested in called the hosts file. Using this file you can override where your computer resolves to find a specified hostname, such as a domain. Altering system files is dangerous business so always grab an original copy and understand how to put it back to how you found it. Just like if you were to take something apart with the intention of putting it back together; keep track of what screws and bolts went where, how the parts came off, and how to put them back. Check out this article from How-To Geek for a more in-depth walkthrough on updating your hosts file.

Updating to a temporary URL is a second option but should only be considered if there’s something, such as SSL issues, that prevent you from using the hosts file method above. As always, keep a backup of your database and understand how to recover if you make a mistake, this should be considered more dangerous than the hosts file method unless you know what you’re doing. Sometimes all that’s required is that you set your site_url and home_url values within the options table of your database, but more likely there are references to your original URL littered throughout your database. Check out this article from WPBeginner for an easy method. If you’re comfortable with the command line you could also use the WP CLI search-replace utility.

Reiterating, once again, updating your hosts file is the best way to preview your site before it goes live on the new server. An alternative, which isn’t 100% but works fairly well, is hosts.cx. You give it an IP and a domain and that’s really it! Some links may be broken in the preview that would otherwise work under normal circumstances.

Plugins

For most WordPress users, especially those with little experience with migrations, using a plugin is a great solution to make this quick and simple. Most of these plugins work by exporting your site on the ‘source’ and importing on the ‘destination’. Be aware that while plugins are simpler to use there are limitations on how big your site can be, even if these limits aren’t advertised. Large sites will commonly timeout during an import or export. In some cases, the better plugins can handle larger sites by breaking the site up into multiple packages, but this is commonly a premium feature. You can see the top migration plugins listed on the WordPress repository.

WP Beginner has taken the time to go into an in-depth comparison on their site. I’ve put together a comparison table for your convenience as well! Hire a Professional

If you’re not sure about migrating your own website and need help, consider your options. A lot of web hosts have tools and support for new customers migrating their website to them, this could make the process easy and give you a comfortable safety net. A lot of hosts offer a migration service, sometimes this service is even free for new and upgrading customers. If you have no other choice you can check out Fiverr or Upwork and hire a freelancer. Be careful with trusting third parties to migrate your site, particularly since you need to give them some sort of access, usually passwords.
